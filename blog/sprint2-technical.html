Margin, Border, and Padding

Nov 22, 2020

When we want to lay out content on a webpage we invariably need to contend with the content's margin, border, and padding. In web development we tend to use an analogy of a box to illustrate how these three properties interact with each other and the content. We use this analogy so often, that it is simply referred to as the Box Model.

The Box Model consists of four concentric squares, with the center-most square representing our content. With our analogy of a physical box the content would be anything you'd store in a box, but in web development this is generally text or an image.

Surrounding the content we have padding. Padding is the space inside the box between the content and the walls of the box. In web development it is one of the ways that we can create space between things. In that way, it is similar to the space between the pizza and the roof of the pizza box, it stops everything from sticking together.

Enclosing the content and the padding, the third square of the Box Model, is the border. The border is used in web development to create an outline around the content of varying thicknesses and styles. In our analogy the border can be thought of as the walls of the box we've put the content in.

The final outermost square of the Box Model is the margin. This is the space between our box, made up of the content, padding, and border, and every other box. If we imagine a few more boxes in our analogy, then the margin is like how tightly or loosely we've stacked our boxes. In web design it is the other way to create space between things, but unlike padding the space created is outside the box.

With these three properties: margin, border, and padding we are able to place content wherever we want to on a web page.
